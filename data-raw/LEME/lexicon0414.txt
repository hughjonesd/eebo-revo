<wordgroup1 type="t" object="definitions">
<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="cord(n)"><f type="r">A Cord, </form> <xpln>or Subtense, is a right line, drawn from the one extre&shy;
mity to the other of an Arch. </xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="right sine(n)"><f type="r">2. A right Sine </form> <xpln>is the half Cord of the double Arch proposed,
and from one extremity of the Arch falleth perpendicularly on the 
<f type="I">Radius<f type="r">, passing by the other end thereof.</xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="tangent(n)"><f type="r">3. A Tangent </form> <xpln>is a right line, drawn from the Secant by one end of
the Arch, perpendicularly on the extremity of the Diameter, passing
by the other end of the said Arch.</xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="secant(n)"><f type="r">4. A Secant </form> <xpln>is the prolonged <f type="I">Radius<f type="r">, which passeth by the upper
extremity of the Arch, till it meet with the sine Tangent of the 
said Arch.</xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="complement(n)"><f type="r">5. Complement </form> <xpln>is the difference betwixt the lesser Arch, and a 
Quadrant, or betwixt a right Angle and an Acute.</xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="complement to a semi-circle(n)"><f type="r">6. The complement to a semi-Circle, </form> <xpln>is the difference betwixt the
half-Circumference and any Arch lesser, or betwixt two right An&shy;
gles, and an Oblique Angle, whither blunt or sharp.</xpln></wordentry>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="versed sine(n)"><f type="r">7. The versed sine </form> <xpln>is the remainder of the <f type="I">Radius<f type="r">, the sine

<section type="preface">
<heading>
A <f type="I">Lexicidion<f type="r"> of some of the hardest
words, that occurre in the discourse of this
institution <f type="I">Trigonometricall.<f type="r">
</heading>

BEing certainly perswaded, that a great many
good spirits ply <f type="I">Trigonometry<f type="r">, that are not ver&shy;
sed in the learned Tongues, I thought fit, for
their encouragement, to subjoyne here the ex&shy;
plication of the most important of those Greek, and Latin
termes, which, for the more efficacy of expression, I have
made use of in this Treatise: in doing whereof, that I
might both instruct the Reader, and not weary him, I have
endeavoured perspicuity with shortnesse: though (I speak it
ingenuously) to have been more prolixe therin, could have
cost but very little labor to me, who have already bin pret&shy;
ty well versed in the like, as may appear by my Etymolo&shy;
gicall dictionary of above twenty seven thousand proper
names, mentioned in the Lemmas of my serveall Volums of 
Epigrams, the words whereof are for the most part abstru&shy;
ser, derived from moe Languages, and more liable to
large, and ample interpretations. However (<f type="I">c&aelig;teris paribus<f type="r">)
brevity is to be preferred; therefore let us proceed to the 
Vocabulary in hand.
</section>

<wordentry type="h"><form lexeme="alternate(a)"><f type="r">Alternat, </form> <xpln><f type="I">is said of Angles, made by a Line cutting two or more pa&shy;
