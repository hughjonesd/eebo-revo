<heading>Scotticisms. </heading>

<section type="Prologue">
<p>
<f type="i">WILL</f> in the first person, as <f type="i">I will walk, we will 
walk</f>, expresses the intention or resolution of the 
person, along with the future event: In the second and 
third person, as, <f type="i">you will, he will, they will</f>, it expresses 
the future action or event, without comprehending or 
excluding the volition. 
</p>

<p>
<f type="i">Shall</f> in the first person, whether singular or plural, 
expresses the future action or event, without excluding 
or comprehending the intention or resolution. But in 
the <f type="i">second</f> or <f type="i">third</f> person, it marks a necessity, and com&shy;
monly a necessity proceeding from the person who 
speaks; as, <f type="i">he shall walk, you shall repent it.</f> 
</p>
<p>
THESE variations seem to have proceeded from a po- 
liteness in the <f type="i">English</f>, who, in fpeaking to others or of 
others, made use of the term <f type="i">will</f>, which implies voli&shy;
tion, even where the event may be the subject of neces&shy;
sity and constraint. And in speaking of themselves,
made use of the term, <f type="i">shall</f>, which implies constraint,
even tho' the event may be the object of choice.
</p>
<p>
<f type="i">Wou'd</f> and <f type="i">shou'd</f> are conjunctive moods, subject to
the same rule ; only we may observe, that in a sen&shy;
tence, where there is a condition exprest, and a conse&shy;
quence of that condition, the former always requires

</page>
<page no="2">


<f type="i">shou'd</f>, and the latter <f type="i">wou'd</f>, in the second and third per&shy;
sons; as, <f type="i">if he shou'd fall, he wou'd break his leg, &c.</f>
</p>
<p>
<f type="i">These</f> is the plural of <f type="i">this</f>; <f type="i">those</f> of <f type="i">that</f>. The for&shy;
mer therefore expresses what is near. The latter what 
is more remote. As, in these lines of the duke of <f type="i">Buck&shy;
ingham.</f>
</p>
<p>
" Philosophers and poets vainly strove, 
" In ev'ry age, the lumpish mass to move. 
" But THOSE 'were pedants if compar'd with THESE, 
" Who knew not only to instruct but please. 
</p>
<p>
WHERE a relative is to follow, and the subject has 
not been mention'd immediately before, <f type="i">those</f> is always 
requir'd. <f type="i">Those observations which he made. Those 
kingdoms which</f> Alexander <f type="i">conquer'd.</f>
</p>
<p>
IN the verbs, which end in <f type="i">t</f>, or <f type="i">te</f>, we frequently o&shy;
mit <f type="i">ed</f> in the preter-perfect and in the participle; as,
<f type="i">he operate, it was cultivate. Milton</f> says, <f type="i">In thought
more elevate</f>; but he is the only author, who uses that
expression.
</p>
<p>
<f type="i">Notice</f> shou'd not be us'd as a verb. The proper
phrase is <f type="i">take notice</f>. Yet I find lord <f type="i">Shaftesbury</f> uses
<f type="i">notic'd</f>, the participle: And <f type="i">unnotic'd</f> is very common.
</p>
<p>
<f type="i">Hinder to do</f> is <f type="i">Scotch</f>. The <f type="i">English</f> phrase is <f type="i">hinder
from doing</f>. Yet <f type="i">Milton</f> says, <f type="i">Hinder'd not Satan to
pervert the mind</f>. Book IX.
</p>
</section>
